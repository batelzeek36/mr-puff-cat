# Product notes

Carried over from `~/Documents/noni/big-island-pet-care-guide.html` plus what's relevant to a new stray (which Noni isn't, since she's an established household cat).

## Flea Rx: Revolution Plus over Bravecto, but ask the vet

The shopping list lists Bravecto Topical first and Revolution Plus as the "or". The noni guide leans the other direction and that opinion still stands:

- **Revolution Plus** (selamectin + sarolaner, topical, monthly): broader coverage including some intestinal worms and ear mites. Topical means less systemic exposure. Selamectin has decades of safety data.
- **Bravecto Topical for Cats** (fluralaner, topical, every 12 weeks): convenient because of the long dosing interval. But fluralaner is in the **isoxazoline class**, which the FDA flagged in 2018 for rare neurological side effects (tremors, seizures). The risk is small but real. The 12-week dosing also means if she has a bad reaction, you can't just skip a month.

For a previously-untreated stray with unknown medical history, the gentler topical with monthly dosing gives you more control. But the vet will have a view based on her actual exam, so this is "ask, don't dictate."

If the vet pushes Bravecto: ask why, and specifically ask whether they've seen neurological reactions in their practice. A good vet will engage with the question. A vet who waves it off without acknowledging the FDA letter is worth being a little skeptical of.

## OTC stopgap if Rx is delayed

- **Advantage II Large Cat** (imidacloprid + pyriproxyfen, topical, monthly): the OTC alternative. Won't cover worms, but kills fleas + IGR for eggs. At PETCO-H or Amazon.
- **Elanco Tapeworm Dewormer** (praziquantel): one tablet for cats over 5 lbs. Use if she's been flea-positive for any meaningful time, since cats get tapeworms from grooming swallowed fleas. Amazon or PETCO-H.

Don't combine Advantage II with Revolution Plus on the same day. Space them by 2 weeks.

## Why IGR matters for the environmental treatment

Adult fleas are only ~5% of any infestation. The other 95% is eggs, larvae, and pupae living in carpet, furniture, and your cat's bedding. An adulticide-only spray (plain pyrethrins, or "kills on contact" labels) wipes out the visible 5% and the 95% hatches over the next 4-8 weeks.

An IGR (methoprene or pyriproxyfen) blocks the molting hormone, so larvae and eggs never become biting adults. **Always check the label for one of these two ingredients before buying.**

Hawaii has no winter to break the cycle, so the re-treatment timing on the back of the can usually under-counts. Plan on re-treating at the **4-month** mark, not the 7-month mark the Adams label suggests.

## Permethrin warning

Permethrin is in many dog flea products and a lot of household sprays. It's lethal to cats, even small exposures. If you treat the house with anything that contains permethrin, the cat needs to be out of the house for ~24 hrs and surfaces need to be dry before she comes back.

The Adams home spray and the Black Flag products listed in shopping-list.md use IGRs + pyrethrins (different from permethrin) and are generally considered cat-safe once dry, but still keep her out until the surfaces are fully dry.

## Bite relief context

Flea bites on humans usually clear in a few days but the IgE-mediated itch can hang around for 1-2 weeks. Hydrocortisone 1% topical handles most of it. Add an oral antihistamine at night if it's keeping you awake. Zyrtec (cetirizine) is non-drowsy in the day, Benadryl (diphenhydramine) at night if you want the drowsy effect to actually sleep.

If you see expanding redness, warmth, or any sign of secondary infection from scratching, that's a "go to the doctor" not a "more hydrocortisone" situation.

## On letting her keep coming inside

The protocol that minimizes risk to your resident Maine Coon, in order:

1. **Before vet results:** keep Mr. Puff out of the bedroom and away from your cat's food/water/litter. Wash hands between handling them. Don't let them share surfaces.
2. **After negative FIV/FeLV:** safe to introduce them slowly. Start with scent swapping (rub a towel on each, swap), then short supervised meetings. Don't rush this; full integration takes 2-6 weeks for most cats.
3. **After flea treatment + 30 days clean:** safe to share spaces freely.

If the FIV or FeLV result is positive, that's a different conversation and the vet will walk you through the options. FIV-positive cats can live full lives with appropriate management; FeLV is more serious. Don't make any decisions before getting the vet's read.

## Big Island specifics worth knowing

- **Little Fire Ants (LFA):** present in Puna. If she's denning outside, treat the area with **Tango bait** (active ingredient methoprene, available at HD-H or via the Hawaii Ant Lab). LFA bites cause cloudy eyes in pets - if you see her squinting, that's the most likely cause.
- **Cane toads:** if she's eating things in the yard at night, a toad lick can be fatal. Symptoms are foaming + drooling within minutes. Rinse the mouth with running water (head down so she doesn't aspirate) and go straight to the vet.
- **Centipedes:** painful but rarely lethal. If she's bitten and just acting sore, watch for 24 hrs.
- **Year-round flea season:** no winter break, so flea prevention is 12 months a year, not seasonal like the mainland.

The full Big Island hazard write-up is in the noni guide (`~/Documents/noni/big-island-pet-care-guide.html`). Don't re-read it for the basics, but it's worth a skim if anything weird shows up.
