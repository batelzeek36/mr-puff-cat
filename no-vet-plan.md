# If we can't get her to a vet

Three real options before you give up, in order of cost / friction. Plus an honest "no vet at all" fallback.

## What's still true even without a vet

- The **ear tip** confirms she's been through TNR previously, which means **she's already spayed**. The biggest risk of an unfixed outdoor female (constant heat cycles, kittens) is off the table. This makes the no-vet path much less crazy than it would otherwise be.
- The ear tip also means she's been trapped before, so a humane trap will probably work on her again. Some TNR cats become "trap-shy" but most don't.
- FIV/FeLV status is the one thing you really can't determine without a blood draw. That's the load-bearing reason to find some way to get her to a vet, even if it takes a few weeks.

## Tier 1: Mobile vet comes to you

Solves the carrier-and-transport problem entirely. Costs ~$100-200 in a house-call fee on top of the actual services (so maybe $300-500 total instead of $200-300).

- M-W and Fri 7:30a-4p, Thu 7:30a-11:30a, weekends closed
- Can do FIV/FeLV snap, fecal float, physical exam, microchip scan, dispense Revolution Plus, all on your lanai

### All Pets Mobile Vet Clinic
- **(808) 935-3400**
- Hilo-based mobile, second option if BI Mobile Vet can't fit you in

**When to call this tier:** Mr. Puff is friendly enough to be touched but you can't realistically get her into a carrier and into a car. Or you don't have a car. Or she's reliably at your place but disappears the moment she sees the carrier.

## Tier 2: Trap her first, then transport

If she won't go in a carrier voluntarily, the standard TNR move is a humane trap. Bait with sardines or rotisserie chicken at the time she normally shows up to eat. She probably remembers being trapped from her original TNR, so it might take a couple of nights of getting her used to eating *near* the trap before you set it.

### Where to get a trap
- **Hawaii Island Humane Society Keaau Shelter**, 16-873 Keaau-Pahoa Hwy. Call them and ask: "Do you loan or rent humane cat traps?" The Oahu HHS does ($75 deposit, $25 fee, refundable on return). HIHS Keaau likely does the same but I couldn't confirm online, so call to verify.
- **PETFIX Big Island** (petfixbigisland.org) sometimes loans traps tied to their clinic events.
- **Buy one** if you'll keep using it: Tomahawk Live Trap (32" model) or similar, ~$60-90 on Amazon. Worth it if you might end up doing this for other cats.

### Trapping tips
- Trap right before the vet appointment, not days in advance. Trapped cats stress fast.
- Cover the trap with a towel after she's in. Calms them dramatically.
- Don't open the trap to "transfer" her to a carrier. Drive her in the trap to the vet and let them handle the transfer.

**When to use this tier:** She won't let you touch her, OR she lets you touch her but panics when you try to confine her. The trap removes the wrestling step.

## Tier 3: Mobile spay/neuter clinic event

Free, but you wait for an event near you and you're locked into their service menu.

### PETFIX Big Island
- petfixbigisland.org for the event schedule
- Holds free clinics in Keaau and Ocean View
- Confirmed: spay/neuter, vaccines, often microchips
- Not confirmed: FIV/FeLV testing. Call before assuming this is included. If they don't, you'd still need a separate visit somewhere for the snap test.

### Hawaii Island Humane Society Community Vet Care (Keaau)
- Lower cost than a regular clinic, by appointment, often weeks out
- Will do FIV/FeLV
- Worth getting on the schedule even if you also pursue Tier 1 or 2 in parallel

**When to use this tier:** Money is the constraint, time isn't. She's stable, you're not in a hurry, and you want to keep her care under $100.

## Tier 4: Honest "no vet at all" plan

This is suboptimal, not impossible. The cost is that **you can never safely integrate her with your Maine Coon**, because you can't rule out FIV/FeLV. She stays an outdoor cat that you feed, full stop. No coming inside the house.

### What you can still do without a vet

**Parasites (all OTC):**
- **Advantage II Large Cat** monthly, topical between the shoulder blades. Imidacloprid + pyriproxyfen. Petco Hilo or Amazon. ~$60 for 6-month supply.
- **Elanco Tapeworm Dewormer** (praziquantel), one tablet for cats over 5 lbs. OTC, no Rx needed. Treats tapeworms from swallowed fleas. Amazon, sometimes Petco. ~$15 per tablet.
- **Panacur (fenbendazole)** for roundworms / hookworms. Sold OTC for goats and dogs in larger sizes. Off-label for cats but commonly used by TNR groups. Dose by weight. Skip if she's asymptomatic.

**Fight wounds / abscesses (the most common outdoor cat problem):**
- If you spot a wound: warm compress, twice daily, until it drains or scabs cleanly. Watch for swelling, heat, or hiding behavior.
- If she goes off food for more than 24 hrs, that's the signal to escalate to one of the tiers above. Cats hide pain hard, and going off food usually means infection.

**Environmental:**
- Adams flea spray for any indoor area she might still drift into (e.g. a screened porch). See `shopping-list.md`.
- Black Flag yard concentrate if she dens under your house.

### What you give up

- **No FIV/FeLV result.** She and your Maine Coon never share space, food bowls, water, or grooming. If your Maine Coon hates this, that's a real cost.
- **No dental check.** Tooth disease is the most common silent issue in older outdoor cats. Untreated dental abscesses can become systemic.
- **No microchip scan.** If she's a lost pet rather than a dumped/community cat, you'll never find her actual owner.
- **No baseline bloodwork.** You won't know about kidney disease, hyperthyroidism, or anything else age-related until it's advanced.

### When this tier is actually fine

- She's clearly an established community cat (lots of people feed her, she has territory)
- She's healthy-looking, eating well, no obvious wounds
- You're providing supplemental food + flea treatment + a dry place to sleep, not trying to make her your indoor pet
- You're prepared to call one of the mobile vets the day she goes off food

This is essentially what colony caretakers do. It's a real model of care, just not the same as adoption.

## Decision shortcut

| Situation | Tier |
|-----------|------|
| She'll let you touch her, just hard logistics | 1 (mobile vet) |
| Won't let you touch her, but eats reliably from one spot | 2 (trap + transport) |
| Money is the only block | 3 (PETFIX or HIHS clinic) |
| She's not really "yours," she's a community cat you feed | 4 (OTC + separation) |

In practice you might do Tier 1 first, fall back to Tier 2, fall back to Tier 4 indefinitely. That's a normal stray-cat care arc on the Big Island.
