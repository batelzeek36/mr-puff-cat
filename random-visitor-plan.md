# The realistic plan: she comes when she comes

If Mr. Puff drops by on her own schedule, can't be confined, and you can't hold her for an appointment, then **stop trying to plan around a vet visit**. The whole "schedule" framing is wrong for this situation. Here's what actually works.

## Reframe the problem

You don't have a stray cat that's *about to become your pet*. You have a **community cat that you feed**. That's a different category and the playbook is different.

In community-cat land:
- The vet visit may never happen, and that's OK
- The flea problem is an **environment + opportunistic treatment** problem, not an on-cat problem
- Your Maine Coon and Mr. Puff stay separated indefinitely (period, no exceptions, no integration arc)
- The bites you're getting are from fleas in your yard / lanai / soft surfaces, not from her sitting on you

That last point is the most important. Killing all the fleas on Mr. Puff while doing nothing about your yard accomplishes basically nothing for your bite problem. The reverse (treating your environment hard while doing nothing to her) actually solves your bite problem.

## What you can do during a random visit

These are all things that take seconds and don't require holding her down.

### Capstar / CapAction (oral, hide in wet food)
- **Active ingredient:** nitenpyram. Kills 100% of fleas on the cat within 30 minutes. Lasts 24 hours.
- **OTC.** No vet needed. Walmart, Petco, Amazon. ~$30 for 6 doses (Capstar brand) or ~$15 for the generic CapAction.
- **Dose:** 11.4 mg tablet for cats 2-25 lbs. Any normal-sized cat fits.
- **How to give:** drop the pill in a tablespoon of wet food (Fancy Feast pate works best, the smell covers the pill). She eats it. Done.
- **Does not prevent reinfestation.** This is not a monthly preventive. It's a "for the next 24 hours she's a non-flea-source on your property" tool.
- **Buy a box. Use one every time she shows up.** Eventually she's coming around with no live fleas, your yard catches up, and you stop getting bitten.

### Advantage II topical (only if she lets you touch her)
- Squeeze the tube between her shoulder blades. Takes 5 seconds.
- Lasts a month. Imidacloprid + pyriproxyfen.
- If she'll let you stroke her along the spine, you can do this. If she won't, skip it and rely on Capstar.

### Tapeworm dewormer in food
- **Elanco Tapeworm Dewormer** (praziquantel), one pill, OTC, ~$15.
- Same trick as Capstar: hide in wet food, she eats it.
- One dose handles tapeworms acquired from swallowing fleas. Repeat in 2-3 weeks if you can.

### Visual flea-comb check (only if she lets you)
- Fine-tooth metal comb, comb the rump and base of the tail. That's where flea dirt collects.
- If you find black specks: smear them on a wet paper towel. If they smear red, that's flea dirt (digested blood), confirming an infestation.
- This is just diagnostic, not treatment. Tells you whether your environmental work is keeping up.

## What to actually fix: your environment

This is the load-bearing part of the plan. The fleas biting you live in your yard, under your house, on your lanai, in any soft surface she touches indoors. They cycle through their lifecycle in 2-4 weeks in Hawaii's climate.

### Yard / under the house (the source)
- **Black Flag Yard Concentrate (32 oz hose-end)** at Home Depot Hilo. Lambda-cyhalothrin + pyriproxyfen 0.125%. Treats up to 5,000 sqft. Spray any area she dens, naps, or hangs out in. **This is probably your highest-impact single purchase.**
- Re-treat every 4 months in Hawaii (the label says longer; humidity shortens IGR life).
- If she dens under your house, treat the crawl-space perimeter heavily. Wear a mask, ventilate.

### Lanai / porch / any sheltered outdoor area
- **Adams Flea & Tick Home Spray (24 oz trigger, fragrance-free)** at Walmart. (s)-methoprene + pyrethrins.
- Spray cushions, mats, anywhere she sits. Re-treat at the 4-month mark.

### Indoors (if she's drifting inside at all)
- Vacuum aggressively (every 2-3 days during active treatment). Dump the canister outside immediately.
- Wash any soft thing she touches in **hot** water weekly. Towels, blankets, throw pillow covers.
- Spray Adams on carpets and furniture. Air for an hour, vacuum, done.

### Yourself
- 1% hydrocortisone cream from Longs Pahoa. Apply to bites as needed.
- Zyrtec (cetirizine) once a day, non-drowsy, knocks down the histamine response.
- The bites should taper off within 2-3 weeks of consistent yard + lanai treatment. If they don't, the source is somewhere you haven't sprayed.

## The long game: maybe-someday vet visit

Don't plan for it, but don't rule it out. The way it usually happens:

1. You feed her every time she shows up. Same spot, same time of day, same routine.
2. After a few months she starts showing up at predictable times.
3. You get her used to eating in or next to a carrier (food inside the carrier, door propped open, no pressure).
4. One day she's relaxed enough that you can close the carrier door behind her.
5. **That's when you call.** Not Pahoa Animal Hospital (waitlist). Big Island Pet Care Center in Keaau, **(808) 966-5402**, and ask if they can fit a trapped stray in same-day. They have a track record of saying yes.
6. Or call **Big Island Mobile Veterinary Care, (808) 731-4027**, and tell them she's contained right now and you need a same-day house call. Premium fee, but they'll come.

This might be three months out. It might be a year. It might never happen. None of those outcomes are failure modes; they're just the range.

## What stays true regardless

- **Mr. Puff and your Maine Coon never share space.** No food bowls in common, no shared water, no shared surfaces, no shared rooms. The Maine Coon is fully indoors during this whole time, and Mr. Puff is fully outdoors-or-lanai. This is non-negotiable until you have an FIV/FeLV result, which might be never.
- **Wash your hands** between handling Mr. Puff (or anything she touches) and touching your Maine Coon.
- **If Mr. Puff disappears for more than 2 weeks**, that's normal community-cat behavior, not necessarily a problem. She has territory and other feeders.
- **If she shows up injured, lethargic, or not eating**, *that's* when you escalate to the same-day mobile vet call. An off-food day is the universal "something is wrong" signal in cats.
