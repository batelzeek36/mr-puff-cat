Top 5 Priorities

All focused on Mr. Puff. He's flea-infested and almost certainly unvaxxed. In order.

  2. Tapeworm dewormer next Heavy flea load = essentially guaranteed tapeworms (cats get them by swallowing fleas while grooming). Elanco Tapeworm Dewormer (praziquantel), one pill in food, ~$15. Add pyrantel pamoate (Nemex-2) for roundworms/hookworms while you're at it.

01The Situation

whats-actually-going-on.md

Mr. Puff is an adolescent ear-tipped community cat with a heavy flea infestation. He drops by on his own schedule, has come inside for full days twice, and likely hasn't been to a vet since (or maybe ever — the ear tip doesn't tell us if he was vaccinated at the same time). The plan is to just treat him at home, with everything available OTC and the vet only as a fallback for emergencies.

  • The ear tip tells you he was spayed/neutered during a TNR cycle. It does NOT tell you his FIV/FeLV status, and it does NOT necessarily mean he got vaccines — budget TNR programs often skip both because of cost.
  • A massive flea load is more than a comfort issue at any age. Heavy infestations can cause flea anemia, and tapeworms are essentially guaranteed via grooming. Adolescent cats handle parasites better than seniors, but a really severe load still drains a cat's red cell count.
  • No recent vet contact means his core vaccine protection (FVRCP, FeLV) is either expired or never existed. This is the big one we can fix at home with OTC vaccines.
  • The resident Maine Coon has been kept successfully separated. No direct contact, no shared spaces during overlap. That stays the rule until anything changes.
Assume status unknown Treat Mr. Puff as if he could be FIV-positive and FeLV-positive for planning purposes. That's why you keep him and the Maine Coon separated, and why you wash hands between handling them.

What you CAN'T do at home

  • FIV/FeLV blood test. Requires venipuncture skill. The load-bearing unknown.
  • Bloodwork (CBC, chemistry panel, T4 thyroid). No DIY path.
  • Dental cleaning, extractions, dental X-rays. Requires general anesthesia.
  • Surgery. Spay is already done (ear tip), but anything else needs a clinic.
  • X-rays, ultrasound. Imaging requires equipment.
Boundary You can do all routine preventive care + minor wound care + symptom-based triage at home. You cannot diagnose or treat internal disease without imaging or labs.

03Mr. Puff Care

opportunistic-otc.md
Senior cat with heavy flea load Older cats handle parasites worse than younger ones. Watch for flea anemia: pale gums (should be pink, not whitish), lethargy, reluctance to move, weak appetite. Severe anemia is one of the few things that justifies a same-day mobile vet call no matter what.

Every visit until the flea load drops (food-disguised)

All hidden in a tablespoon of Fancy Feast pate. The smell covers any pill.

  • Capstar / CapAction (nitenpyram) 11.4 mg. Kills 100% of fleas in 30 min, lasts 24 hrs. With a heavy infestation, dose every visit for the first few weeks. ~$15 generic 6-pack. Amazon · Petco Hilo

First round of dewormers (do once now, then every 3 months)

With a flea infestation this heavy, tapeworms are basically certain. Roundworms and hookworms are common in outdoor cats too. Treat empirically.

  • Elanco Tapeworm Dewormer (praziquantel) 23 mg, one dose. Amazon
  • Pyrantel pamoate liquid (Nemex-2) for roundworms/hookworms. 1 mL per 10 lbs. Repeat in 2 weeks (catches the next lifecycle), then every 3 months.

If she lets you touch her

  • Advantage II Large Cat topical between shoulder blades, monthly. Imidacloprid + pyriproxyfen. ~$60 for 6-pack at Petco Hilo.

Vaccines you administer (cold-chain shipped)

Subcutaneous, between shoulder blades, 1 mL with a 22g needle. The skill: lift skin tent, slide needle in at the base, inject, withdraw. YouTube has good demos from Revival and Jeffers.

Visual screening (no diagnostics, just observation)

  • Eyes: clear vs cloudy. Cloudiness = LFA bite (most likely on Big Island), URI, or aging cataract. Squinting = pain or LFA.
  • Mouth: lift the lip if she'll let you. Healthy gums are pink. Rot smell = dental disease.
  • Coat: part fur on the rump. Flea dirt looks like pepper, smears red on a wet tissue. Round patches with raised borders = ringworm.
  • Weight: lift her weekly, get a sense for whether she's getting lighter. Weight loss is always a flag.
  • Ears: dark crumbly debris = mites. Clean with mineral oil on a cotton ball.
  • Wood's lamp ($15 on Amazon) for ringworm screening. M. canis glows green under UV in ~50% of cases.

04Heavy Flea Load + Probably Unvaxxed

two-things-to-fix.md

The two real problems with Mr. Puff specifically: the load on him right now, and the gap in his protection. Both fixable at home.

Flea-anemia gum-check (do this now)

The one urgent thing to rule out before settling into the routine. If you can lift his lip even briefly, look at the gums above the upper canines:

  • Salmon pink · capillary refill in <2 sec when you press = healthy, proceed with the OTC plan
  • Pale pink, slow refill = mild anemia. Capstar + dewormer + monitor over the next week. If it doesn't improve, escalate.
  • White or grayish = URGENT mobile vet today, do not wait

What "probably unvaxxed" actually means

If he was ear-tipped during TNR, he might have gotten one round of FVRCP at the same time, or he might not have. Many low-cost / volunteer TNR efforts skip vaccines entirely because the cost-per-cat doubles. Either way, immunity from a single shot at TNR-time has long faded.

The diseases that matter for an outdoor cat without protection:

  • Panleukopenia (feline parvo) — environmental, deadly to unvaccinated cats, present everywhere
  • Calicivirus + herpesvirus (URIs) — common, miserable, not usually deadly in healthy adolescents but rough
  • FeLV — saliva-transmitted between cats, eventually fatal in many infected cats. Outdoor cats are the highest-risk population.

FVRCP covers the first two. FeLV needs a separate vaccine. Both are OTC and DIY-able.

Adolescent vaccine schedule (start now)

  • FVRCP: first dose → booster 3-4 weeks later → annual
  • FeLV: first dose → booster 3-4 weeks later → annual
  • Skip rabies (Hawaii is rabies-free, must legally be given by a vet anyway)
  • Watch for reaction in the 24 hrs after each dose: swelling at injection site, lethargy, vomiting. Rare but real. If it happens, call a mobile vet.

05Keeping Them Separated

no-contact.md

The Maine Coon and Mr. Puff have stayed separate so far. That's the win. The goal is to keep it that way until you have an FIV/FeLV result on Mr. Puff (which might be a long time from now).

What's working, keep doing

  • Bedroom door closed when Mr. Puff is inside.
  • Different rooms during overlap.
  • Wash hands between handling them.

Soft-edge precautions

  • Don't share bowls. Even if they're in different rooms, don't rotate the same food/water dishes between them. Get a second set for Mr. Puff and label them.
  • Capstar Mr. Puff at every visit (already in the priorities). Stops fleas hopping off him onto surfaces the Maine Coon might encounter later.
  • If Mr. Puff sleeps inside, give him his own dedicated spot (laundry room, mudroom, screened lanai with a blanket) so the contamination is contained, not spread across the whole house.
Status No exposure means no Maine Coon vaccine catch-up needed for Mr. Puff's sake specifically. Keep his routine FVRCP boosters current per the AAFP schedule (every 1-3 years), but no urgency-driven additions.

06Bedroom Decon

flea-egg-reservoir.md

Mr. Puff has slept on the bed twice with a heavy flea load. Eggs hatch in 2-14 days in Hawaii's climate. The bed is a reservoir.

  1. Strip the bed. Hot wash everything: sheets, pillowcases, comforter, mattress pad. If anything is dry-clean only, take it now.
  2. Vacuum the mattress. Both sides if possible. Edges and seams especially. Dump canister outside immediately into a sealed bag.
  3. Light spray of Adams home spray on the bare mattress. Let dry fully (1-2 hours, ventilated). Then put a fresh sheet on.
  4. Mattress encasement (Allerzip or similar, ~$30) is worth it. Zips around the whole mattress, traps any surviving eggs.
  5. Vacuum the bedroom floor daily for 2 weeks, dump canister outside each time. Flea pupae hatch in response to vibration; vacuuming triggers them, then sucks them up.
  6. Wash blankets and throw pillows weekly going forward, hot water, regardless of whether she's been in.

07Yard & Home

environment-first.md

The yard (the source)

  • Black Flag Yard Concentrate 32 oz hose-end at Home Depot Hilo. Pyriproxyfen IGR + lambda-cyhalothrin. Treats 5,000 sqft. Spray any area she dens, naps, or hangs out in. Highest-impact single purchase.
  • Re-treat every 4 months in Hawaii (the label says longer; humidity shortens IGR life).
  • Diatomaceous earth (food-grade) along the foundation perimeter as a non-toxic mechanical barrier.

Lanai / porch / sheltered outdoor

  • Adams Flea & Tick Home Spray (24 oz trigger, fragrance-free) at Walmart Hilo. (s)-methoprene + pyrethrins.
  • Spray cushions, mats, anywhere she sits. Re-treat at 4 months.

Indoor surfaces

  • Vacuum aggressively (every 2-3 days during active treatment). Dump canister outside immediately.
  • Wash any soft thing she touches in hot water weekly.
  • Spray Adams on carpets and furniture. Air for an hour, vacuum, done.
  • Flea trap (light + sticky pad, $10 on Amazon) as a monitoring tool. If you're catching fleas a week after spraying, treatment isn't holding.
Permethrin warning Permethrin is in many dog flea products and household sprays. Lethal to cats, even small exposures. If you treat with anything containing permethrin, both cats need to be out for ~24 hrs and surfaces fully dry. The Adams + Black Flag products listed here use IGRs + pyrethrins (different from permethrin) and are cat-safe once dry.

08For You

human-health.md

Bite and scratch protocol

  • Wash with soap and water immediately for at least 60 seconds.
  • Iodine or chlorhexidine, not alcohol. Alcohol burns; iodine actually kills bacteria.
  • Pasteurella multocida is the bacterium in most cat mouths. Cat bites infect fast (12-24 hrs). Symptoms: spreading redness, warmth, throbbing. Urgent care for amoxicillin/clavulanate.
  • Deep bite near a joint or tendon = ER, same day.

Bartonella henselae (cat scratch fever)

  • Vectored by fleas. Aggressive flea control is the prevention.
  • Symptoms in humans: low fever, swollen lymph node near the scratch, malaise. Usually self-limiting but can persist.
  • Swollen lymph nodes a few weeks after a scratch → doctor, ask about doxycycline.

Ringworm

  • Round, scaly, itchy patch with a raised border (often shaped like a ring) = likely ringworm from her.
  • OTC clotrimazole or miconazole cream (athlete's foot section) twice daily for 2-4 weeks.
  • If it spreads or doesn't respond → doctor for oral antifungals.

Flea-bite relief

  • 1% hydrocortisone cream from Longs Pahoa
  • Zyrtec (cetirizine) non-drowsy in the day
  • Benadryl (diphenhydramine) at night if you want the drowsy effect to actually sleep

General hygiene

  • Wash hands after handling her, before handling food or your face.
  • Don't let her lick your mouth or open wounds. Bartonella also transmits oral-mucosal.
  • If pregnant or immunocompromised, the toxoplasmosis risk from her feces is real — use gloves for any cleanup, or have someone else handle it.

09When to Escalate to a Vet

no-vet-but-emergency.md

Heavy parasite load + unvaxxed = real risk of complications even on an otherwise healthy young cat. If you're on the fence, call.

Same-day mobile vet triggers

  • Pale or white gums (flea anemia) — do not wait
  • Off food for >24 hrs (universal "something is wrong" signal in cats)
  • Lethargy, hiding for more than a day
  • Visible deep wound, especially with swelling, heat, or smell
  • Cloudy eyes, squinting, pawing at face (LFA bite or eye trauma)
  • Foaming at mouth, drooling heavily (cane toad ingestion)
  • Difficulty breathing, open-mouth breathing
  • Stops walking normally, dragging a limb, sudden weakness in the back end
  • Severe vaccine reaction in the 24 hrs after a DIY shot (face swelling, vomiting, collapse)

Schedule-it-soon triggers (not same-day, but don't keep punting)

  • Persistent diarrhea after dewormers haven't fixed it
  • Sneezing or eye discharge that lasts more than 10 days
  • New lump that doesn't go away in 2 weeks
  • Slow weight loss over a month
