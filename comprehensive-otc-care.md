# Comprehensive no-vet care plan

She's been inside for full days twice now and slept on your bed. The original "keep her separated until vet results" line is already broken. Pivot to **harm reduction at the surface level**, not prevention. This is the deeper playbook.

## The new reality

- She's a part-time house cat, not a stray you're considering helping. Two overnight visits with no escalation panic from her means she's probably going to keep doing it, and the visits will get longer.
- Your Maine Coon has been exposed. Anything Mr. Puff carries that's transmitted via shared surfaces (FeLV, URI viruses, ringworm, fleas) has had a chance to transfer.
- The "ear tip" tells you she was spayed and microchipped during a TNR cycle. **It does NOT tell you her FIV/FeLV status.** Budget TNR programs in Hawaii usually skip the snap test because positive cats are still released under standard TNR ethics. Assume status unknown.

## What a vet does that you can substitute at home

| Vet service | OTC alternative | Honesty rating |
|-------------|-----------------|----------------|
| FVRCP vaccine | OTC vaccine + DIY subq injection | Solid - widely done by rescues, breeders, farms |
| FeLV vaccine | OTC vaccine + DIY subq injection | Solid for routine; FeLV positive cats need full vet workup |
| Flea/tick control | Capstar oral + Advantage II topical | Equivalent to most vet products |
| Tapeworm dewormer | Elanco praziquantel OTC | Identical drug to vet version |
| Roundworm/hookworm dewormer | Pyrantel pamoate (Nemex-2) OTC | Identical drug |
| Wound flushing | Saline + dilute chlorhexidine | Same protocol |
| Abscess management | Warm compress until drains | Same protocol; antibiotics still needed for systemic spread |
| Ear mite check | Visual + mineral oil cleaning | Reasonable for screening |
| Ringworm screening | Wood's lamp ($15 Amazon) + DTM culture ($15) | ~50-80% sensitive; positive = treat empirically |
| Empirical antibiotics | Fish/aquarium antibiotics (amoxicillin, doxycycline) | Pharmaceutically identical, dosing risk; emergency use only |

## What a vet does that you CANNOT substitute

- **FIV/FeLV blood test.** Requires venipuncture skill. Some saliva-based home tests exist (Witness FeLV-FIV) but accuracy is degraded. This is the load-bearing unknown.
- **Bloodwork** (CBC, chemistry panel, T4 thyroid). No DIY path. Need a draw and a lab.
- **Dental cleaning, extractions, dental X-rays.** Requires general anesthesia.
- **Surgery.** Spay is already done (ear tip), but anything else needs a clinic.
- **X-rays, ultrasound.** Imaging requires equipment.
- **Sedated exams.** For a fractious cat where you need to look in the mouth or do diagnostics.

So the realistic boundary: you can do all routine preventive care + minor wound care + symptom-based triage at home. You cannot diagnose or treat internal disease without imaging or labs.

## For Mr. Puff: opportunistic OTC stack

Each visit, in priority order. All food-disguised in Fancy Feast pate.

### Every visit (or every 24-48 hrs while she's inside)
- **Capstar / CapAction (nitenpyram) 11.4 mg** in food. Kills 100% of fleas in 30 min. Lasts 24 hrs. Buy a 6-pack. ~$15 generic.

### Once when first establishing care, then every 3 months
- **Elanco Tapeworm Dewormer (praziquantel) 23 mg** in food. One dose. ~$15.
- **Pyrantel pamoate liquid (Nemex-2 or generic)** for roundworms/hookworms. Dose: 1 mL per 10 lbs. ~$15 for a bottle. Repeat at 2 weeks (catches the next lifecycle round), then every 3 months.

### When she lets you touch her
- **Advantage II Large Cat** topical between shoulder blades, monthly. Imidacloprid + pyriproxyfen. ~$60 for 6-pack.
- This is for the days you can't get her to take a Capstar.

### Vaccines (you administer)

You buy these, store cold, give yourself. Subcutaneous, between shoulder blades, 1 mL. Use a 22g needle. The skill is "lift skin tent, slide needle in at base of tent, inject, withdraw." YouTube has good demos from Revival Animal Health and Jeffers.

**FVRCP (the core vaccine for panleukopenia + the URI viruses):**
- Brand options: Spectra Feline 3 (Boehringer), Felocell 3 (Zoetis), Nobivac Feline 1-HCPCh (Merck)
- Source: Tractor Supply, Jeffers Pet (jefferspet.com), Revival Animal Health (revivalanimal.com), Valley Vet
- Cost: $5-10 per dose
- Schedule: prime dose + booster in 3-4 weeks + annual after that (some protocols stretch to every 3 years)
- This is the highest-yield vaccine. Panleukopenia is a deadly cat-to-cat virus and FVRCP also covers the URI viruses she could give your Maine Coon.

**FeLV (Feline Leukemia):**
- Brand options: Nobivac Feline 2-FeLV (Merck), Spectra Felo3 with FeLV
- Source: Revival Animal Health, sometimes Jeffers
- Cost: $10-15 per dose
- Schedule: prime + booster in 3-4 weeks + annual
- Caution: ideally you'd snap-test for FeLV BEFORE vaccinating, because vaccinating a positive cat is pointless. But if testing isn't available, vaccinating empirically is acceptable harm reduction.

**Rabies:**
- Skip. Hawaii has no rabies. Vaccine must legally be given by a vet anyway.

### Screening (no diagnostics, just visual)
- **Eyes:** clear vs cloudy. Cloudiness in one or both eyes = LFA bite (most likely on Big Island), URI, or aging cataract. Squinting = pain or LFA.
- **Mouth:** lift the lip if she'll let you. Healthy gums are pink, not red or pale. Tartar buildup is yellow-brown. Smell breath (rot smell = dental disease or worse).
- **Coat:** part the fur on the rump. Flea dirt looks like pepper, smears red on a wet tissue. Round patches of hair loss with raised borders = ringworm.
- **Weight:** lift her once a week, get a sense for whether she's getting lighter. Weight loss in cats is always a flag.
- **Ears:** dark crumbly debris = ear mites. Clean with mineral oil on a cotton ball.
- **Wood's lamp** ($15 on Amazon) for ringworm screening. M. canis (the most common cat ringworm) glows green under UV in about 50% of cases. Negative doesn't rule out, but positive is meaningful.

## For your Maine Coon: this is now the high-priority workstream

Mr. Puff's exposure to your cat is already done. You can't prevent it retroactively. What you can do is harden your Maine Coon's defenses now.

### Verify vaccines and DIY-boost if needed
- **When was the last FVRCP?** If more than 1 year ago for a cat under 7, or more than 3 years ago for any age, boost now. OTC FVRCP is fine for this. Same brands as above.
- **Has the Maine Coon ever had FeLV vaccine?** If no, start the series now. Two-shot prime + booster, then annual. Mr. Puff has been on the bed; FeLV transmits via shared surfaces, mutual grooming, and shared food bowls.
- The annual cost of FeLV vaccine for one indoor cat is ~$10-15. Insurance against a meaningful risk.

### Flea control on the Maine Coon
- Capstar dose now (immediate knockdown of any fleas she picked up).
- Then either monthly Advantage II topical, or a longer-acting Rx if you want to call a mobile vet for one visit just for this.
- She likely has fleas now even if you can't see them. Treat empirically.

### 60-90 day symptom watch
- Sneezing, eye discharge, congested breathing -> URI from Mr. Puff. Usually self-limiting in 7-14 days in vaccinated cats. Escalate if she stops eating.
- Lethargy + weight loss + pale gums -> FeLV anemia. Escalate immediately.
- New lumps -> FeLV-associated lymphoma is rare but the most serious risk. Escalate immediately.
- Diarrhea or vomiting -> probably parasites; deworm with pyrantel and watch.
- Skin patches, scaly hair loss -> ringworm. Treat with miconazole cream from the human pharmacy section (athlete's foot cream); if widespread, call a vet.

### Reduce ongoing shared-surface exposure
- Block bedroom door when Mr. Puff is inside. Bed is the highest-stakes shared surface.
- Two food stations: Maine Coon's food on a high counter Mr. Puff can't reach, or behind a baby gate the Maine Coon can jump.
- Two water bowls if you can manage it.
- Two litter boxes in different rooms if Mr. Puff starts using one inside.

You can't fully separate them now without locking your Maine Coon in a room, which isn't a reasonable life. Aim for "minimum shared surfaces" instead of "zero."

## For your bedroom: decontamination

This is now flea-egg territory.

- **Strip the bed.** Hot wash everything: sheets, pillowcases, comforter, mattress pad. If anything is dry-clean only, take it to a cleaner now.
- **Vacuum the mattress.** Both sides if possible. Edges and seams especially. Dump the canister outside immediately into a sealed bag.
- **Light spray of Adams home spray** on the bare mattress. Let dry fully (1-2 hours, ventilated). Then put a freshly-washed sheet on.
- **Mattress encasement** (Allerzip or similar, ~$30) is worth the spend. Zips around the whole mattress, keeps any surviving eggs trapped, easy to wipe down.
- **Vacuum the bedroom floor daily for 2 weeks**, dump canister outside each time. Flea pupae hatch in response to vibration; vacuuming triggers them, then sucks them up.
- **Wash blankets and throw pillows weekly going forward**, hot water, regardless of whether she's been in the room.

## For the rest of the house

- Adams home spray on all carpet, all upholstered furniture, all soft surfaces. Air for an hour, vacuum, done. Re-treat at 4 months.
- **Flea trap** (light + sticky pad, $10 on Amazon) is a useful monitoring tool. Set one up in the room she's most often in. If you're catching fleas a week after spraying, your treatment isn't holding.
- Dispose of any cheap soft items she's heavily contaminated (old throw pillow, dog bed she claimed) rather than fighting to clean them.

## For the yard

- **Black Flag Yard Concentrate** (32 oz hose-end, Home Depot Hilo). Pyriproxyfen IGR + lambda-cyhalothrin. Treat the yard, especially anywhere she dens or naps. Re-treat every 4 months in Hawaii's humidity.
- **Diatomaceous earth** (food grade) along the foundation perimeter as a non-toxic mechanical barrier for fleas crossing into the house.

## For yourself

### Bite and scratch protocol
- **Wash with soap and water immediately** for at least 60 seconds.
- **Iodine or chlorhexidine, not alcohol.** Alcohol burns and dehydrates the wound; iodine actually kills bacteria.
- **Pasteurella multocida** is the bacterium in most cat mouths and lives on their claws. Cat bites infect fast (within 12-24 hours). Symptoms: spreading redness, warmth, throbbing. **If this happens, urgent care for amoxicillin/clavulanate.** This is more important than people realize.
- Deep bite that goes near a joint or tendon = ER, same day.

### Bartonella henselae (cat scratch fever)
- Vectored by fleas. Aggressive flea control is your prevention.
- Symptoms in humans: low fever, swollen lymph node near the scratch, malaise. Usually self-limiting but can persist.
- If you develop swollen lymph nodes a few weeks after a scratch, see a doctor and ask about doxycycline.

### Ringworm
- If you develop a round, scaly, itchy patch with a raised border (often shaped like a ring), that's likely ringworm from her.
- OTC clotrimazole or miconazole cream (athlete's foot section) twice daily for 2-4 weeks.
- If it spreads or doesn't respond, see a doctor for oral antifungals.

### General hygiene
- Wash hands after handling her, before handling food or your own face.
- Don't let her lick your mouth or open wounds. Bartonella also transmits oral-mucosal.
- If you're pregnant or immunocompromised, the toxoplasmosis risk from her feces is real - use gloves for any cleanup, or have someone else handle it.

## When to escalate to a vet anyway

Even with a no-vet plan, these are the signals that mean call a mobile vet today:

### Mr. Puff symptoms
- Off food for more than 24 hours (universal "something is wrong" signal in cats)
- Lethargy, hiding for more than a day
- Visible deep wound, especially with swelling or smell
- Cloudy eyes, squinting, pawing at face
- Foaming at mouth, drooling heavily (cane toad ingestion)
- Difficulty breathing, open-mouth breathing
- Stops being able to walk normally

### Your Maine Coon symptoms
- Same list as above, escalate faster (you have better baseline)
- Plus: any new lump, persistent sneezing, weight loss

### Your symptoms
- Bite that becomes red/warm/swollen (even small)
- Fever or chills after a bite
- Spreading rash from a scratch
- Persistent ringworm patch >4 weeks of OTC treatment

## What you can't fully eliminate

Be honest about this:

- **Undetected FIV/FeLV in Mr. Puff -> potential transmission to Maine Coon.** Mitigated by: vaccinating Maine Coon for FeLV, minimizing shared bowls, watching for symptoms. Not eliminated.
- **Undetected dental disease.** No OTC fix. Could be causing her pain right now and you wouldn't know until she stops eating. Only flag is bad breath + dropping food. The day she does become catchable, dental is the first thing to ask about.
- **Undetected internal disease** (kidney, thyroid, cancer). Common in older outdoor cats. Will manifest eventually as weight loss or behavior change. That's your trigger to escalate.
- **Bartonella in your household.** Even with aggressive flea control, you may have already been exposed. The risk is moderate, the consequences are usually mild.

## The 1-year horizon

If nothing dramatic happens, here's what a year of this looks like:

- Mr. Puff comes around increasingly often. Eventually she's a 50-50 indoor/outdoor cat.
- You've given her 2-3 FVRCP doses, 1-2 FeLV doses, monthly Capstar/Advantage II.
- Your Maine Coon is fully vaccinated for FeLV by now and has had no symptoms.
- Your bite frequency dropped to zero by month 3 from environmental control.
- One day you close the carrier door behind Mr. Puff, drive to BIPCC in Keaau, and finally get the FIV/FeLV result + dental check + bloodwork. Total cost: $300-400.
- The vet either confirms what you've been doing is right, or flags something you didn't catch.

This is a real and reasonable trajectory. Many community cats end up "owned" this way - not from a single decision to adopt, but from gradual integration that the cat herself drives.
