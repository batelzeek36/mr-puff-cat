Koi/Aquaculture-Grade Powder

"100% pure, no fillers" ›

Sold for koi pond treatment, but the underlying powder is just praziquantel. Many serious cat colony caretakers and breeders dose from these because they're cheap by orders of magnitude. None are USP-certified, but the "100% pure no fillers" claim is real and consistent across vendors.

Cost-per-cat-dose math

A 23 mg cat dose comes from 0.023 g of powder. So:

  • Smith Creek 100 g / $94.95: 100 g ÷ 0.023 g = ~4,348 doses = $0.022/dose
  • Aqua Meds 100 g / $129.99: ~4,348 doses = $0.030/dose
  • Wholesale Koi 100 g / $102.67: ~4,348 doses = $0.024/dose

The cheapest finished tablet is ~$3-5. The bulk powder is 100-200x cheaper per dose.

The catch with koi-grade powder
  1. No USP certification or COA paperwork. Vendors say "100% pure" but no third-party testing receipt. Probably fine in practice (competition keeps quality up, plus pharmacy-grade praziquantel is cheap to manufacture), but you can't prove it.
  2. No excipient profile. None claim added flavoring, lactose, magnesium stearate. Unlikely to matter at cat doses; xylitol (the worst-case excipient) is not used in any of these.
  3. Dosing math is on you. Need a 0.001 g resolution scale (~$25 on Amazon). Can't eyeball.
  4. Solubility is low. Praziquantel is poorly water-soluble. To dose by suspension, dissolve in a small amount of ethanol/DMSO/vodka first, then mix with food. Or just stir powder directly into wet food.
The "fish-grade is unsafe for cats" myth cats.com and JustAnswer warnings about "fish-grade praziquantel is unsafe for cats" are technically misleading. The underlying drug is the same. The actual hazard is dosing math errors (someone confuses the per-gallon water-treatment dose with an oral mg/kg dose) and unverified purity. If you measure correctly with a real scale and trust the vendor, the koi-grade powder is the same praziquantel as in the Elanco tablet.

Compounding Pharmacy Bulk API

Rx-channel only ›
  • API suppliers: PCCA (Professional Compounding Centers of America), Medisca, Spectrum Chemical, Letco Medical.
  • Access: These suppliers sell only to licensed compounding pharmacies, not to consumers. You can't open an account directly.
  • What this means for you: if you want compounding-pharmacy-tier praziquantel, get a vet to write a prescription, then have a compounding pharmacy fill it. Wedgewood Pharmacy, Diamondback Drugs (now merged with Wedgewood), Roadrunner Pharmacy, Stokes Pharmacy, Mixlab, Pace Pharmacy will fill flavored chews, tuna oil suspensions, transdermal gels, or custom-strength capsules.
  • Cost: higher than OTC ($30-80 for a flavored chew bottle), but quality assurance is maximum. The compounding pharmacy is licensed, audited, USP <795> compliant.

Useful if you want to combine praziquantel + something else (e.g. with mirtazapine for an inappetent cat, or in a transdermal gel for a cat that won't swallow).

China Direct (Alibaba / Made-in-China)

SKIP unless running a colony ›
  • Suppliers: Miracle Pharmaceutical, Xi'an Sonwu Biotech, Xi'an Chen Lang, Fengchen Group, Jiayi, Dingyanchem.
  • Specs: All claim USP/EP/BP standards at ≥99% purity, CAS 55268-74-1.
  • Wholesale prices: Roughly $30-100 per kg in 1-25 kg bulk orders. So in theory, a kg of "USP grade" praziquantel for ~$50 = ~43,000 doses = $0.001/dose.
Don't do this
  1. No way to verify their COA paperwork. They send a piece of paper that looks like a COA. It might be real. There is no recourse.
  2. Customs. Importing pharmaceutical APIs requires DEA/FDA paperwork that you don't have. Praziquantel isn't controlled, but it's regulated, and bulk imports by individuals get held or seized regularly.
  3. Minimum order quantities are usually 1 kg+. You don't need 43,000 cat doses.
  4. Quality variance. Some Chinese API manufacturers are world-class (the global supply chain depends on them). Some are sketchy. Without an industry relationship you can't tell which.
This is the route for someone running a TNR colony of 50+ cats with budget pressure, not for one stray.

Why "Food-Safe" Is a Slightly Off Framing

grades that actually exist ›

"Food-safe (or close to it) sources of praziquantel powder" — worth being clear about what that actually means:

  • Praziquantel is an FDA-regulated drug, not a food additive. There is no "food grade" of praziquantel because it isn't approved for inclusion in food. The grades that exist are USP (US Pharmacopeia), EP (European), BP (British), and informal "100% pure" claims from aquaculture vendors.
  • "Food-safe" in the casual sense (i.e. "I'm comfortable putting this in my cat's food") is a function of: (1) purity of the active, (2) absence of harmful excipients (xylitol is the big one to avoid), (3) accurate dosing.
  • All the koi-grade "100% pure" powders meet (1) and (2) in practice but don't have paperwork to prove it.
  • Sacred Purity, Medisca, PCCA, and Chinese manufacturers all claim USP grade and have a COA. Sacred Purity is the only one a consumer can buy directly without an Rx.
  • The Elanco / Drontal / Jeffers tablets are made from USP-grade API by definition (FDA approval requires it). They're the most "food-safe" option even though the framing is a bit of a category error.

Dosing from Bulk Powder

if you go the powder route ›

The math

  • Cat dose: 5 mg/kg orally (label dose). Some sources go up to 10 mg/kg; the Elanco label is effectively ~5 mg/kg for a midweight cat (23 mg ÷ ~5 kg).
  • Margin of safety: ~40x. Acute toxicity in cats kicks in at 50-100 mg/kg parenteral (ataxia, depression). 200 mg/kg parenteral is fatal. Oral has a wider margin still.
  • Practical dose for a 5-11 lb cat: 23 mg = 0.023 g.
  • Practical dose for a 12-15 lb cat: ~35 mg = 0.035 g.

Tools you need

  • 0.001 g resolution jewelry scale (Amazon, ~$25). Cheap kitchen scales aren't sensitive enough.
  • Small empty gelatin capsules (size 4 or 3, Amazon, ~$10/1000) if you want to capsule doses in advance.
  • A dedicated weighing dish or weigh paper. Not your cat's food bowl.

Method

  1. Tare the scale with the weighing dish.
  2. Measure 0.023 g of powder.
  3. Either (a) capsule it for later, or (b) mix directly into ~1 tbsp of wet food.
  4. Praziquantel is bitter. Strong-smelling pâté (Fancy Feast salmon, tuna, anchovy) hides it best. Watch her eat it; cats sometimes refuse the bowl after one suspicious bite.

Repeat protocol

Once now, repeat in 2-3 weeks (catches a second round of tapeworm proglottids that hatched after the first dose), then every 3 months as long as she's flea-positive or hunting.

Combo with pyrantel

Pyrantel pamoate liquid (Nemex-2 or generic, sold OTC) is 4.54 mg/mL. Cat dose is 5-10 mg/kg, so a 10 lb (~4.5 kg) cat gets ~5-10 mL. Give pyrantel 30 minutes before or after praziquantel; they don't interact, but separating them helps if she vomits one of them so you don't lose both. Or just buy Jeffers Worm Buster and skip the math.

Safety, Contraindications, the Catch

things to actually know ›
  • No antidote. If you overdose, you support and wait. Margin of safety is wide enough that this is essentially a non-issue at correctly-measured doses, but it's a reason not to "round up to be safe."
  • Don't use on kittens under 6 weeks. No safety data, hepatic metabolism isn't mature.
  • Don't give parenteral (injectable) praziquantel orally, or vice versa. Different formulations. Droncit injectable is concentrated and stings; oral formulations aren't designed for injection.
  • Pregnancy: single-active praziquantel is considered safe; combo products (Drontal, Jeffers Worm Buster) haven't been thoroughly studied in pregnancy and labels say to avoid them. If Mr. Puff is somehow pregnant despite the ear tip (rare but TNR mistakes happen), use Elanco Tapeworm Dewormer alone.
  • Don't substitute dog dewormers for cats unless the label explicitly says cats are OK. PetArmor 7-Way is dog-only; right ingredients but wrong ratio. Sentry Worm-X Plus similarly dog-only.
  • Drug interactions: minimal. Cimetidine (Tagamet) raises serum levels; phenytoin lowers them. Neither is a likely concern.
  • Renal/hepatic disease: dose-reduce or skip entirely. The drug is hepatically cleared.

What I'd Actually Do for Mr. Puff

the recommendation ›

Given the existing comprehensive-otc-care.md says she's a part-time house cat with unknown round/hook/tape status, on a flea-heavy lot:

  1. First treatment: Order a 12-pack of Jeffers Worm Buster ($39.99 shipped, 3-5 days to Pahoa). One tablet hidden in Fancy Feast pâté on the next visit. Repeat in 2 weeks. This handles tape + round + hook in one product, OTC, cat-safe ratios, no math. Best ROI for a single stray.
  2. Maintenance every 3 months: another Jeffers Worm Buster tablet. The 12-pack lasts a year for one cat at quarterly dosing, or 6 months at every-2-month dosing.
  3. If she becomes a true-resident long-haul cat and you're also dosing the Maine Coon: either keep buying Jeffers (~$80/year for both cats at quarterly dosing) or bite the bullet on Smith Creek Prazi Power 100 g + Nemex-2 liquid (~$130 total) which lasts ~4,300 cat doses of praziquantel and the equivalent of years of pyrantel. Only worth this if you're confident in the dosing math and have a 0.001 g scale.
  4. Don't bother with Sacred Purity, compounding pharmacy USP API, or China-direct unless you're scaling up to a colony or have a specific quality-assurance requirement. Two cats don't need that level of supply chain.

1-cat lifetime cost comparison

One 10 lb cat, quarterly tapeworm dosing, for the rest of her life:

  • Elanco 3-pack route: 4 tabs/year × $5/tab = ~$20/year, forever.
  • Jeffers Worm Buster route: 4 tabs/year × $3.33/tab = ~$13/year, forever.
  • Smith Creek 100 g bulk: $94.95 once = ~1,100 years of cat doses. Effectively free per dose, but you're locked into DIY measurement forever.

The OTC tablet routes are well-priced enough that the bulk-powder math only really makes sense at colony-scale or multi-pet households.
